Speaker 2: Exactly, yes. It’s a fascinating development. The initiation sites of replication seem to impact where these fragments end.

Speaker 1: But wait, aren’t termination zones supposed to be predetermined by the DNA sequence itself? How does the origin firing alter that?

Speaker 2: That's the point of contention. The traditional view was that sequence dictated everything. But recent studies indicate that the timing and location of origin firing can actually influence the termination.

Speaker 1: Alright, but if that's true, wouldn't that mean the entire model of replication needs revisiting? I mean, the implications are huge!

Speaker 2: Precisely! That’s why this is such a significant claim. It suggests a dynamic adaptability in the replication process we hadn’t considered before.

Speaker 1: But what about the supporting evidence? I mean, have they shown this effect consistently across different organisms or cell types?

Speaker 2: Well, that’s where the debate intensifies. The initial studies were mostly in yeast, which is a model organism. But there are whispers of similar findings in mammalian cells, though those results aren’t published yet.

Speaker 1: So, we’re basing this on preliminary findings then. What if it's just a species-specific phenomenon?

Speaker 2: That's a fair point. It could be. But it opens up a huge area for research regardless. If it pans out, we could be looking at a fundamental shift in how we understand DNA replication.

Speaker 1: I see. So, the crux of the disagreement is whether this is an anomaly or a universal principle. It’s going to take some serious evidence to settle this one.

Speaker 2: Absolutely. It’s an exciting time for molecular biology, though, isn’t it? So much to explore and potentially rewrite.
